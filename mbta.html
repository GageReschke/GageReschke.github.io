Overview

This project was a cross-disciplinary policy and systems proposal developed as part of a team at Northeastern University. The MBTA has been identified as one of the most dangerous metro systems in the country — with ongoing safety failures including poor emergency communication systems, platform hazards, and a chronic funding gap that limits the agency's ability to modernize.

Our team researched the problem, identified root causes, and developed a phased implementation plan to improve safety while working within the agency's financial constraints.

The Problem

The MBTA faces a compounding crisis: safety infrastructure is aging and underfunded, while a large debt burden limits capital investment. Key issues identified through our research included poor emergency communication systems, inadequate platform safety measures, insufficient incident response protocols, and a structural funding gap that makes large-scale upgrades difficult without new revenue sources.

Our Approach

We analyzed five primary sources including FTA inspection reports, MBTA Public Utilities reports, and financial overviews from 2022–2024 to build a data-backed case for our proposal. From there we developed a three-phase implementation roadmap:

01
Immediate Digital Solutions
Deploy mobile emergency alerts and AI monitoring for real-time incident detection. Assess safety gaps and prioritize key interventions. Train staff on AI analytics and emergency response protocols.
02
Research & Stakeholder Collaboration
Analyze incident reports and refine solutions against best practices from peer metro systems. Launch pilot programs and gather feedback from MBTA officials, city planners, and the public.
03
Infrastructure Enhancements
Develop a comprehensive project plan integrating digital and physical upgrades — platform barriers, lighting, and signage. Secure funding and align with city transportation initiatives.

Funding Strategy

A core challenge of the project was proposing solutions that were financially viable given the MBTA's constrained capital budget. We identified several realistic funding mechanisms including MBTA capital improvement budgets, state transportation grants, city infrastructure funds, and — most creatively — increased tax revenue from up-zoning along transit corridors. The Heath Street green line extension was used as a case study, where up-zoning could generate substantial tax revenue that flows back to transit operations.

Skills Applied

  • Data collection and analysis — gathered transit performance data, incident reports, and financial documents to identify trends and support strategic recommendations
  • Cross-functional team coordination — managed timelines, logistics, and deliverables across all project phases as project lead
  • Technical writing and stakeholder communication — synthesized complex research into structured reports and a final presentation delivered to stakeholders
  • Systems thinking — approached a multi-stakeholder infrastructure problem by breaking it into phased, actionable components with realistic constraints
  • Data visualization — produced a 3D scatter plot analysis of route vs. gated entries vs. time of day to identify usage patterns relevant to safety staffing

Engineering Takeaway

This project reinforced that engineering problems don't exist in isolation — real systems have financial, political, and human constraints. Designing a solution means understanding all of them. The phased approach we developed mirrors how complex infrastructure projects are actually executed: start with low-cost, high-impact digital interventions, validate with pilots, then scale physical upgrades once stakeholder buy-in and funding are secured.
